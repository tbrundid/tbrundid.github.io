About

I'm a writer and graduate student based in Chicago, Illinois. I am pursuing a masters degree in Geography from Northeastern Illinois University. In addition to my degree studies, I am a research assistant at the National Hazard Planning Center within the American Planning Association. In both arenas, my work is centered at the intersection of critical earth systems science, environmental planning, and economic geography. Outside of geography, I am a locally well-established electronic musician/beatmaker with several releases on a variety of physical formats.

  • 2006-2010

    University of Illinois at Urbana Champaign

    I received my B.A in Print-Editorial Journalism from the UIUC. For my final editorial project I covered a controversial school redistricting that, were it not for family activists, would have resulted in an unneccessarily long and inconvenient commute for a large number of low income students of color in Champaign County. This project was my first encounter with GIS, decision support, and spatial analysis (I had NO idea I'd ever come back to it!). In my extracurricular time as an Illini, I hosted the most listened to campus hip-hop show, Beats and Rhymes Radio on WPGU 107.1 FM; I was the co-chair of the Urbana-Champaign Hip Hop Congress where I put on shows, hosted summits, and recorded local artists; I also kept in shape as a member of the nationally ranked Illinois Men's Ultimate team.

  • 2010-2016

    Sev Seveer

    Although I started making beats in high school and continued through college, during this time support for my beats began to pick up, allowing me to play SHOWS (Fresh Roasted Chicago, Push Beats, sold out Schubas with THEEsatisfaction, and many more), FESTIVALS (2x SXSW, Beat Swap Meet, Kimball House Rock) and even a MUSEUM EXHIBIT celebrating electronic music at the Toledo Museum of Contemporary Art. I have several digital releases and one vinyl 12", Smithereens— a study of post-impressionism that explores the possibilities of "sonic-Pointillism", also known as punctualism. I am a proficient and curious sound designer who prefers vintage hardware sampling units (Akai MPC 2000, 2000xl, MPC60, Boss SP-202, 303, and 404), have working experience as a recording engineer, and many production credits for local, national, and international artists as well as labels.

  • Fall 2016

    Return to Academia

    After a period of feeling unfulfilled while also in need of a less volatile career plan, I decided to go back to school. Unsure of what to study, I enrolled with Northeastern Illinois University's Carruthers Center for Inner City Studies, NEIU's historically black campus in the Bronzeville neighborhood of Chicago. As a born and raised Chicagoan it was fascinating to be acquainted with black history in ways beyond even my own household education. I dove into the early (albeit racist) University of Chicago-inspired theories of urban land use and settlement, and began to read deeply into the historical relationship between African Americans and nature.

  • Spring 2017-now

    A Geographer Emerges

    While seeking help from the NEIU geography department for help with a paper on gentrification, I met Dr. Dennis Grammenos, who told me I would make a better geographer than sociologist. I hadn't realized the geography tools that I had picked up along the way through my interests and also, technical background. After some heavy reading, I was convinced and soon transferred programs. During this time I was also granted an incredible opportunity to conduct research at the American Planning Association— hand in hand with my graduate program— adding skills I never knew or thought I had. Now after dozens of books and hundreds of articles, sleepless nights in front of ArcGIS, and one incredible trip to AAG, I am beginning my final thesis and considering PhD programs.

NLCD Error

Things aren't always what they seem...

In a dasymetric mapping project that explored the advantages of joining cadastral and census data, I demonstrated the potential conflict between National Land Cover Database (NLCD) surveys and census boundaries. Here you can see a subdivision neighborhood of Dallas, Texas, in the Ash Creek watershed.Heavy canopy cover has resulted in the misclassification of this area as deciduous forest (reclassified here as forest/grassland). A simple example of why it is important to scrutinize the relationship between NLCD data and the built environment.
