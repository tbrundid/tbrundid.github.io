About

I'm a writer and graduate student based in Chicago, Illinois. I am pursuing a masters degree in Geography from Northeastern Illinois University. In addition to my degree studies, I am a research assistant at the National Hazard Planning Center within the American Planning Association. In both arenas, my work is centered at the intersection of critical earth systems science, environmental planning, and economic geography. Outside of geography, I am an electronic musician/beatmaker with several releases on a variety of physical formats.

  • 2006-2010

    University of Illinois at Urbana Champaign

    I received my B.A in Print-Editorial Journalism from the UIUC. For my final editorial project I covered a controversial school redistricting that, were it not for family activists, would have resulted in an unnecessarily long and inconvenient commute for a large number of low income students of color in Champaign County. This project was my first encounter with GIS, decision support, and spatial analysis (I had NO idea I'd ever come back to it!).

  • 2010-2016

    Sev Seveer

    Support for my music picked up during this time, allowing me to play shows (Fresh Roasted Chicago, Push Beats, sold out Schubas with THEEsatisfaction, and many more), festivals (2x SXSW, Beat Swap Meet, Kimball House Rock) and a MUSEUM EXHIBIT celebrating electronic music at the Toledo Museum of Contemporary Art.

  • Fall 2016

    Return to Academia

    After a period of feeling unfulfilled while also in need of a less volatile career plan, I decided to go back to school. Unsure of what to study, I enrolled with Northeastern Illinois University's Carruthers Center for Inner City Studies, NEIU's historically black campus in the Bronzeville neighborhood of Chicago. As a born and raised Chicagoan it was fascinating to be acquainted with black history in ways beyond even my own household education. I dove into the early (albeit racist) University of Chicago-inspired theories of urban land use and settlement, and began to read deeply into the historical relationship between African Americans and nature.

  • Spring 2017-now

    A Geographer Emerges

    While seeking help from the NEIU geography department for help with a paper on gentrification, I met Dr. Dennis Grammenos. I hadn't realized the geography tools that I had picked up at the Carruthers Center and also, my technical background in music production which benefits much of the GIS work that I do. After some heavy reading, I was convinced and soon transferred programs. During this time I was also granted an incredible opportunity to conduct research at the American Planning Association— hand in hand with my graduate program— adding skills I never knew or thought I had.

NLCD Error

Things aren't always what they seem...

In a dasymetric mapping project that explored the advantages of joining cadastral and census data, I demonstrated the potential conflict between National Land Cover Database (NLCD) surveys and census boundaries. Here you can see a subdivision neighborhood of Dallas, Texas, in the Ash Creek watershed.Heavy canopy cover has resulted in the misclassification of this area as deciduous forest (reclassified here as forest/grassland). A simple example of why it is important to scrutinize the relationship between NLCD data and the built environment.

Catastrophe Bond Distribution

This map represents the first step of my thesis research. Catastrophe bonds (cat bonds) are finance vehicles created to leverage insurance loss by hedging disaster risk in the market economy, so as to provide greater capacity for relief. Cat bonds are offered for a wide array of events including severe weather, earthquakes, and most recently, wildfire, and pandemic disease. Regardless of type, this mechanism involves a complex and contentious marriage of scientific modelling and market investment principles. This research focuses on cat bonds for hurricane and tropical storm events, and, the ways by which climate modelling epistemologies are negotiated with market investment principles. This map uses feature blending (Subtraction, addition, and dodge). The feature layers are underlain by a vector heatmap, used to illuminate regions of concentrated (or sparse) catastrophe bond issuance.

This is a rough sketch of data that I have begun gathering for my thesis research. Mapping these securities and their implications has proven to be a challenge; this map, as a living document, has helped me to understand the extent of what is needed for future data representation
